Before the algorithm

Access is uneven before any algorithm is involved.

The system spends less on patients who already had less access. Trained on biased data. Designing a sound but unfounded solution.

Obermeyer, Z., Powers, B., Vogeli, C., & Mullainathan, S. (2019). Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations. Science, 366(6464), 447–453.
1.1 personal hook.
The case in one breath
  • One real algorithm.
  • Used by US hospitals to flag patients for high-risk care management.
  • Ranked patients by predicted cost. The assumption: costlier means sicker.
  • That assumption broke along racial lines.
  • Black patients were under-flagged. The system spends less on them at the same level of illness.
Obermeyer, Z., Powers, B., Vogeli, C., & Mullainathan, S. (2019). Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations. Science, 366(6464), 447–453.
1.2 case in one breath.
The three figures, locked

Three separate findings.

84%
Bias reduction
What it isThe researchers kept the model but changed its target to combine cost with direct measures of illness. Missed illness in Black patients fell from 48,772 excess conditions to 7,758, an 84% drop. Far more genuinely sick Black patients would now be flagged for extra care.
What it is notA rise in chronic conditions in any group.
Obermeyer et al. (2019), p. 453.
17.7% → 46.5%
Black enrolment share
What it isA what-if. If the score tracked health instead of cost, the share of Black patients flagged for automatic referral would rise from 17.7% to 46.5%. The size of that jump is the size of the problem with the old target.
What it is notThe same finding as the 84%. Two different experiments, two different numbers.
Obermeyer et al. (2019), abstract and Fig. 1B.
$1,801
Less spending per year
What it isAt the same level of illness, the system spent $1,801 less per year on each Black patient. The model learned that pattern and read "cheaper" as "healthier."
What it is notA health gap. An access gap.
Obermeyer et al. (2019), p. 450.
1.3 three figures.
The frame

Right algorithm. Wrong target.

A cost stand-in launders the system's existing inequality into a number that looks neutral.
Data: Obermeyer et al. (2019), Fig. 1A. Curves illustrative; the 4.8 and 3.8 values at the top 3% automatic-referral threshold are exact.
Risk score is the algorithm's prediction of who will cost the most. The hospital used it to decide who gets extra care. At the same score, Black patients were actually sicker; the score under-counted their health needs.
1.4 · Frame thesis with bias curve.
Stakeholder 1 of 10 · the origin node

Vendor data scientists

Built the model right. Picked the outcome wrong.
Cost was the stand-in for health. But health is biomarkers, not dollars. Same score, worse health:
  • Blood sugar  +0.6 pts in Black patients  →  ~30% higher all-cause mortality
  • Blood pressure  +5.7 mmHg in Black patients  →  ~7.6% higher all-cause mortality
  • Kidney (creatinine)  worse in Black patients  →  worse kidney function
  • Anemia (hematocrit)  worse in Black patients  →  more anemia
Matched-score gaps: Obermeyer et al. (2019), p. 449 (blood sugar, blood pressure). Kidney and anemia: direction from Fig. 2D, 2E; matched-score values not reported.

The thesis
Profit, society, and the model are three pillars in tension.
  • The feasible solution does not have to optimize profit; most solutions can lessen their profit margin to protect against societal harm.
  • It's up to us to design the process: governance must be a pre-flight mandate, not a compliance retrofit.
